# Science Communication Digest

Paraphrased actionable checklists. Dutch craft terms are kept where useful. This is a **style guide only** — never use it as a factual source.

---

## Ch 1 — Introduction

- Treat science communication as a learnable craft, not innate talent.
- Beta topics need extra translation work: abstract concepts must become tangible.
- Do not hide behind “content over form”; better presentation reaches a wider audience.
- Interview experts for process tips, but remember: reading rules ≠ becoming Feynman.

---

## Ch 2 — Why communicate?

- Explain societal relevance: people won't fund what they don't understand.
- Go where non-scientists are; don't only preach to colleagues.
- Connect everyday objects (GPS, payments, medicine) to underlying science.
- Share beautiful ideas the way you'd recommend a book or song.
- Ignore snobbery about media visibility; outreach ≠ lower scientific quality.
- Deeper insight: explaining forces you to clarify your own thinking.
- Inspire the next generation; show that science is exciting, not just exam material.
- Do it because it's fun — passion is contagious.

---

## Ch 3 — Choosing a subject *(high priority for articles)*

- Avoid stories only insiders care about; test whether outsiders would keep reading.
- **Audience first**: who reads this, and why now?
- Pick topics you'd happily talk about at a party.
- Train yourself: note observations, collect anecdotes, read news critically.
- **News hook**: tie your expertise to something already in the news or daily life.
- “News” for popular science ≠ journal publication; timing and relevance matter more.
- Repeat topics when context changes (season, current events, new angle).
- Connect to daily life before diving into theory (friendship networks → network theory).
- Exceptions that always work: dinosaurs, astronomy, and (carefully) gross/funny angles.
- Don't try to explain your entire field in one short piece.

---

## Ch 4 — Structure *(high priority)*

- **Reject the zandloper** (hourglass) for popular pieces: don't start with long background.
- **Inverted pyramid** for news: main point first, then detail.
- Answer the **5 w's** early: who, what, where, when, why (+ often *how*).
- Default structure: **pakkend begin → onderhoudend midden → helder einde**.
- Opening must **fascinate before it explains** — curiosity before exposition.
- Strong openers:
  - Surprising question
  - Human story behind the discovery
  - Vivid image / scene
- Weak openers: “Reeds de oude Grieken…”, abstract definitions, roadmap summaries.
- Middle: one idea per ~400-word block; cut digressions even if interesting.
- Build goodwill with accessible blocks before harder material (Gould technique).
- Offer “breathing room” in the middle (anecdote, lighter paragraph).
- End clearly: reader must feel closure; columns can echo the opening.
- Advanced: walvis (Hollywood arc) or omgekeerde piramide once basics are solid.

---

## Ch 5 — Writing the article *(high priority)*

- **Talk to the editor first** before drafting for newspapers/magazines.
- Use **kleurplaatmethode**: outline themes/blocks and word budget before sentences.
- Expect heavy cutting; experienced writers still delete a lot.
- **Afhakers**: anything that makes the reader stop — cut or rewrite.
- **Ritme**: vary sentence length; ask after each sentence what the reader wants next.
- **Lekkere woorden**: place strong words at sentence end; avoid tangled word order.
- **Lakmoesproef**: read aloud after an hour, in a noisy room; stumble = rewrite.
- Get a non-expert reader; if they don't understand, the text failed — not them.
- **Five basisregels**:
  1. Prefer active over passive (*lijden* constructions)
  2. Cut filler; avoid dated words (*doch*, *voornemens*, *door middel van*)
  3. Avoid stacked prepositions — split into two sentences
  4. Keep verb parts together (no tangconstructies)
  5. Avoid nominalization (*het analyseren van*) — use verbs
- Writing is a craft; a short journalism workshop pays off.

---

## Ch 6 — Presentations *(brief)*

- Rehearse extensively; timing matters.
- PowerPoint: slides support you, not replace you; avoid bullet overload.
- Use objects, demos, interaction where possible.
- Humor helps if natural; never forced.
- Seek feedback; watch great speakers (e.g. Royal Institution lectures).
- Prepare for nerves; simplify when live.

---

## Ch 7 — Metaphors *(high priority)*

- Use metaphors to bridge abstract → everyday experience.
- **When to use**: concept far from daily life; process invisible to outsiders.
- **When not to**: metaphor takes longer to explain than the concept itself.
- Must match audience worldview (caravans, dresses, sports — not insider physics jokes).
- Invent by deeply understanding the process first, then finding parallel domains.
- One strong image can carry a paragraph; optionally weave metaphor words throughout.
- Metaphors also explain **how science works** (exploration, rules of the game).
- Deflect silly questions with gentle analogies (Olympics vs “I'm bad at math”).

---

## Ch 8 — Word choice *(high priority)*

- One wrong word (*graaf*, *kettingbreuk*, *model*) can derail readers.
- Assume **less jargon knowledge** than you think (atoms vs molecules, etc.).
- Explain a term on first use; drop it if a plain word works.
- Tone: neither condescending (“everyone knows…”) nor intimidating (“only 7 people understand”).
- Watch **double meanings** in Dutch/English (*significant*, *theorie*, *robot*, *experiment*).
- Avoid forbidden/dated words and bureaucratic phrasing.
- Formulas: only if they earn their space; translate what they *mean*.
- Precision vs accessibility: match precision to audience; math words often have everyday senses.
- **Mag je liegen?** Simplify, don't distort; white lies in framing are a judgment call — never fake data.

---

## Ch 9 — Images & graphs *(high priority for PubMed chart)*

- Images sell the story; pick colorful, concrete visuals over generic stock.
- Respect copyright; credit sources.
- **Graphs must support your main point** — decide what the number *means* first.
- Prefer counts over bare percentages when percentages obscure scale.
- Default charts: line, bar. Avoid pie charts, 3D charts, scatterplots for general readers unless taught.
- **Lieggrafieken**: never truncate y-axis to exaggerate; label axes; start at zero when comparing magnitude.
- Remove software defaults (Excel/Keynote often mislead).
- Less is more: one clear message per figure.
- For infographics, hire a designer.

---

## Quick pre-publish checklist

1. **Hook**: Does paragraph 1 create curiosity (not background)?
2. **5 w's**: Are who/what/why clear early?
3. **Structure**: One main idea per section; clear ending?
4. **Afhakers**: Any sentence you'd skip if tired?
5. **Jargon**: Every technical term justified and explained once?
6. **Metaphors**: Do they clarify faster than they confuse?
7. **Active voice & lekkere woorden**: Read aloud — any stumbles?
8. **Graphs**: Honest axes, labeled, supports one point?
9. **Facts**: All claims tied to references — not to this style guide.
